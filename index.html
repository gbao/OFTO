1. Background

1.1. Foundation and regulatory framework

The Offshore Transmission Owner (OFTO) regime was introduced in 2009 to govern private investment in high-voltage transmission assets connecting offshore wind generation to the national electricity grid. The Department for Energy and Climate Change and the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority (Ofgem) developed the regime jointly.

The regime addresses four objectives: securing electricity supply, accelerating decarbonisation, minimising consumer costs, and delivering resilient transmission infrastructure. It manages upfront financial risk in offshore transmission development.

Legislative Framework

Electricity Act 1989: Established licensing requirements for electricity transmission, extended to offshore assets.

Energy Act 2004: Inserted section 6C into the Electricity Act, enabling competitive tenders for offshore transmission licences.

Electricity (Competitive Tenders for Offshore Transmission Licences) Regulations 2015: Governs the operational tender process for OFTO licence awards.

1.2. Ofgem's objectives for the OFTO regime

The key objectives of the competitive tender process are:

(i)

Deliver transmission infrastructure to connect offshore generation, on a timely basis and ensure that OFTOs are robust and can deliver Transmission Services successfully over the licence period;

(ii)

Provide certainty and best value to consumers though the competitive tender process; and

(iii)

Attract new entrants to the transmission sector.

Source: Ofgem

1.3. The Authority's role: mandate and mechanism

Ofgem designs, implements, and administers the OFTO framework. It acts under statutory duties to protect consumer interests, promote competition, and ensure efficient energy infrastructure.

1.3.1. Statutory mandate

Consumer Protection

Statutory duty to maximise green energy deployment while securing best value for consumers.

Licensing Authority

Powers under Energy Act 2004 to award offshore transmission licences via competitive tender.

Enforcement

Monitoring and enforcement of licence conditions and regulatory compliance.

1.3.2. Regime implementation

Competitive Tendering

Multi-stage tender process (EPQ, ITT, PB, SB) to appoint licensees. Winner selected by lowest Tender Revenue Stream (TRS).

Price Control

TRS incorporated into 25-year licence as regulated annual revenue.

Asset Valuation (FTV)

Determines Final Transfer Value for transitional projects based on economic and efficient costs. Actively disallows inefficient expenditure.

Tender Management

Manages tender process, subject to delays from construction-stage technical and commercial issues.

2. Financial analysis and valuation

2.1. Scenario analysis

Bull Case: IRR 10-12%

Accelerated offshore wind deployment drives multiple competitive tenders; favorable regulatory reviews enhance allowed returns; institutional capital scarcity supports pricing discipline

Base Case: IRR 8-10%

Stable tender pipeline maintains competitive but rational bidding; regulatory framework continues with minor adjustments; moderate inflation supports real return preservation

Bear Case: IRR 6-8%

Delayed offshore wind projects reduce tender frequency; increased competition from infrastructure funds compresses returns; regulatory pressure on allowed returns

2.2. Risk assessment

Regulatory Risk (Medium)

Potential for adverse regulatory reviews affecting allowed returns; mitigated by strong political support for renewable infrastructure and 20-year revenue agreements

Operational Risk (Low-Medium)

Asset performance and maintenance cost uncertainty; managed through comprehensive O&M contracts and availability incentive mechanisms

Market Risk (Low)

Competition intensity affecting future tender pricing; offset by high barriers to entry and limited qualified bidder universe

3. Investment thesis

Recommendation: BUY

We recommend pursuing OFTO investment opportunities. The recommendation is based on superior risk-adjusted returns, regulatory stability, and strategic alignment with energy transition themes.
